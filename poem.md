# The House Beside the House

I tried to tell your life
without stealing it.
Not facts—
a faithful approximation,
like tracing a mountain from memory
and getting the light right
if not every stone.

You spoke of your father
building a house right next door,
and I saw it:
not replacement,
not departure,
but a second balcony
from which to admire
the first thing his hands once made.

Two homes,
one maker,
one long act of care.
He tends both,
sets things in order,
chooses who sweeps the floor,
who keeps the windows clear,
who remembers where the cups belong.
Strange, yes—
but exactly the shape of his wanting.

Love can be recursive:
a room inside a room,
a life that watches itself being lived.
Like standing near royalty,
not because of crowns,
but because devotion
has its own ceremony.

And maybe that is the point:
not the app,
not the archive of what gets through,
but the intention—
to enter another person's weather,
and, with trembling accuracy,
say:
I could not be you,
but I stood as close as I could.
